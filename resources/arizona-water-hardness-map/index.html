Arizona Water Hardness Map: GPG Levels by City

Reviewed by Solit Zitnik, Water Quality Specialist with 15+ years of experience · Effective April 21, 2026 · 7 min read
Quick Answer

Arizona water hardness ranges from 14 to 23 grains per gallon (GPG) across major cities. Phoenix averages 17-20 GPG, Mesa 18-22 GPG, Glendale 19-23 GPG, and Tucson 14-18 GPG. Anything above 7 GPG is classified as "very hard" by the Water Quality Association (WQA), making nearly every Arizona city's water among the hardest in the United States.

What Water Hardness Means in Arizona

Water hardness is the total concentration of dissolved calcium and magnesium in water, measured in grains per gallon (GPG) or milligrams per liter (mg/L) of calcium carbonate equivalent. One grain per gallon equals 17.1 mg/L. Arizona's tap water consistently registers in the highest category on the WQA classification scale because the state's three primary source waters (Colorado River, Salt River Project, and groundwater) all flow through carbonate-rich geology before reaching the tap.

The U.S. Geological Survey reports that Arizona has some of the hardest municipal water in the country, with portions of the Phoenix metro exceeding 350 mg/L (about 20 GPG). For homeowners, this hardness translates directly into scale on faucets, shortened appliance lifespans, soap that won't lather, and plumbing that calcifies from the inside out. WQA Hardness Classification Scale

The Water Quality Association uses a five-tier scale to describe hardness so consumers can compare results across labs and water sources. Every Arizona metro tap supply falls into the top tier.

  • Soft: 0 - 1 GPG (0 - 17 mg/L)
  • Slightly Hard: 1 - 3.5 GPG (17 - 60 mg/L)
  • Moderately Hard: 3.5 - 7 GPG (60 - 120 mg/L)
  • Hard: 7 - 10.5 GPG (120 - 180 mg/L)
  • Very Hard: Over 10.5 GPG (over 180 mg/L)

Source: Water Quality Association (WQA) Glossary of Terms, 2024 edition.

Why Is Arizona Water So Hard?

Arizona water is hard because the state's three major source waters all originate in or pass through limestone, dolomite, and other carbonate-bearing geology that dissolves calcium and magnesium into solution. The Colorado River, which feeds the Central Arizona Project (CAP) canal serving Phoenix and Tucson, picks up minerals across 1,450 miles of the Colorado Plateau before reaching Lake Havasu. The Salt River Project (SRP) draws from watersheds in the Mogollon Rim and Tonto basin, both rich in carbonate rock.

Groundwater wells in the Salt River Valley aquifer add even more hardness because the water has been in contact with mineralized rock for thousands of years. According to the Arizona Department of Environmental Quality, groundwater in central Arizona basins commonly contains 200-400 mg/L of calcium carbonate before any treatment.

Health and Home Effects of Very Hard Water

Hard water is not classified as a health hazard by the EPA, which lists hardness only as a secondary (aesthetic) contaminant. However, the cumulative cost to homeowners is substantial. A 2024 Water Quality Research Foundation study found that water heaters operating on untreated 20-GPG water lose 24% of their thermal efficiency within five years and fail an average of 30% sooner than those running on softened water.

Common signs of very hard water in Arizona homes include white scale rings inside kettles and on showerheads, soap scum that won't rinse off glass shower doors, dingy laundry that feels stiff after washing, and dry, itchy skin after bathing. If you notice any of these, schedule a free water test to confirm the GPG level at your address.

How to Treat Arizona's Very Hard Water

The only way to reduce hardness at the whole-home level is ion-exchange softening, which swaps calcium and magnesium ions for sodium or potassium ions across a resin bed. Standard single-tank softeners go offline during regeneration (typically 2 a.m.) and lose roughly 5-10% softening efficiency between cycles. Dual-tank systems alternate between two resin tanks so the home always has soft water on demand, which is why most Phoenix-area specialists recommend them for water above 15 GPG.

Seasonal and Source-Blend Variation

Arizona water hardness varies seasonally because municipal utilities adjust the source-water blend in response to drought conditions, demand, and treatment plant capacity. During summer peak demand, Phoenix typically increases the proportion of CAP Colorado River water (which runs roughly 600-700 mg/L TDS and similar hardness) to relieve SRP reservoirs. In winter, the blend shifts back toward SRP (lower TDS, slightly softer). The result is that hardness can swing 2-3 GPG between January and July at the same address, and homeowners often notice scale building faster in summer months.

EPCOR-served communities like parts of Sun City, Anthem, and outlying Chandler tend to run higher hardness than core Phoenix because the supplier draws more heavily from groundwater wells that have been mineralizing for thousands of years. The cumulative cost of untreated very-hard water in Arizona is substantial. The Water Quality Research Foundation's 2024 study estimated that an average four-person Phoenix household spends roughly $1,200 per year in measurable losses: $300-$400 in extra detergent and soap, $200-$300 in shortened appliance lifespans, $200-$300 in energy efficiency loss for water heaters, and $200-$300 in fixture replacement and plumbing maintenance. Frequently Asked Questions

What is the average water hardness in Phoenix, AZ?

Phoenix tap water averages 17 to 20 grains per gallon (GPG), or roughly 290 to 342 mg/L of calcium carbonate. The WQA classifies anything above 7 GPG as "very hard," so Phoenix water is well into the very-hard category.

Which Arizona city has the hardest water?

Glendale typically reports the hardest water in metro Phoenix at 19 to 23 GPG, followed closely by Mesa and Peoria at 18 to 22 GPG. Tucson is the softest major Arizona city, averaging 14 to 18 GPG because of its higher proportion of recharged aquifer water.

Is hard water in Arizona dangerous to drink?

Hard water is not a health risk per the EPA, which classifies hardness as a secondary (aesthetic) standard. However, hardness above 7 GPG damages plumbing, water heaters, and appliances, and leaves mineral residue on skin, hair, and dishes.
